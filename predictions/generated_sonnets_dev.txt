Thou blind fool, Love, what dost thou to mine eyes
That they behold and see not what they see?
They know what beauty is, see where it lies,
And yet dost thou invent a new term
That in it lies concealed all that doth live,
And calls it time, which in truth it is dead?
For thou art both deceiver and merchandiser
And time doth but mend the outward and make it live
More perfect and lasting than flowers or men.
O, that love, what a mansion have those eyes
Which for thy sweet knowledge would see the very place
Which thy false friend hid in time to make thee happy!
  The blind do not see that which the sight tells them.
  Thou hast the advantage of thyself, for thou know'st
  Time is not made, nor can it ever be, thine own.

In faith, I do not love thee with mine eyes,
For they in thee a thousand errors note;
But 'tis my heart that loves what they despise,
And that love in me doth this augury keep.
For if mine eye were but blind, thinking on thy face,
Thy beauty in me should be my witness;
For 'tis in my soul that I write this account.
O, what a mansion have those eyes doth invent
For scope and beauty's scope to view!
Look what their blessed thoughts have in view
Of thee and of thy days to come make record!
  All nations have these records, and yet none
  To thee these records add but in small kingdoms.
  Save to my people this, that I love thee still, and tell it.
  Then thou mayst make this memorial a charter,
  For I, who love thee so, will keep it, even unto the end.

Love is my sin, and thy dear virtue hate,
Hate of my sin, grounded on sinful loving.
O, but with mine compare thou thine own state,
And thineOTHER with myself dost live!
Thou that art so strongly grounded in me,
Who for myself am strongly minded against thee,
Makes a common good, which is not mine but thine.
And so true is love that, though in me it thrive,
It in thine also, to thee give due import.
  "Fair in what it is, true in what it thinks,"
  says Shakespeare. "Fair in what it is, true in what it doth think."
  This is what true love says in mine ear:
  "Thy right is mine, and mine is thee."

Lo, as a careful huswife runs to catch
One of her feathered creatures broke away,
Sets down her babe, and makes all swift dispatch
To her swift food, that is, her husband's dove.
But since thy beauty so swift grows,
And since every wing her beauty lends,
The care of such a brood affords room
But not all that is wantonly spent.
So when at last, full grown, she flies
From her burrow to the open air,
The victor being many, his pride sad;
But being asked where he found such a beauty,
His answer was, "In thy fur."
  This was the first flower that bore my name;
  Then others followed, but I was by far the best.
