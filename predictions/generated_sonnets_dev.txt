Thine eyes I love, and they, as pitying me,
Knowing thy heart torment me with disdain,
Have put on black, and loving mourners be,
One upon another's eyes, to grieve thee there.
Yet 'gainst this my deepest love, which methinks
Thy all the world hath, is quite hid,
And I see it, and they with it, in thy heart.
Herein is my proof that thy love is true;
In this I know it false, and 'gainst it a grief.
  Hence, 'fore I learn this, 'fore I make thee depart.
  This leave I will not part with, though thou wilt part with me.


1
Beshrew that heart that makes my heart to groan
For that deep wound it gives my friend and me.
Is 't not enough to torture me alone,
To hear that same heart in my song praise
That very same fool which doth so grossly deceive?
O, how much more doth my grief than water flow
To that grief which in my grief doth take!
Let my friend Benjamine be with me,
And yet his sorrow doth but make me wail,
Whilst my grief doth add more pain to my grief!
  O, sell your friend, sell him not so dear,
  For that deep wound doth make him so dear still.


2
So, now I have confessed that he is thine
And I myself am mortgaged to thy will,
Myself I'll forfeit, so that other mine
May by thy adverse action be taken.
In other words, he is mine, and I am his,
So that, by thy will, I am quite sold,
Seeking no other gain than that which thou dost pay.
In other words, I am thy slave, and he is a laborer;
Both work together for thy good, and thereby find
A common expense, which, by thy will, I shall take
And by that be a better borrower.
  Thus we two find our acquaintance
  Where both find our society by thy consent.


3
Whoever hath her wish, thou hast thy will,
And will to boot, and will in overplus.
More than enough am I that vex thee still,
Than that thou mayst by my love make me suffer
Without my consent; for to thee consent is not given.
O, what a happy title do I find,
Happy to have thy love, happy to have my consent,
And yet contented to be so!
--Happy to have nothing, happy to have much!
  Then, happy to be, happy to be gone!
  Then, happy to be, happy to be gone!


4
If thy soul check thee that I come so near,
Swear to thy blind soul that I was thy will,
And will, thy soul knows, is admitted there.
If, on the other hand, thou swear,
Thou mak'st thy soul promise that I return,
Then, I pray, swear to thy soul that I came from thee.
If it swear another, then thou hast thy promise;
If it tell no such thing, then thou gav'stst me thine own word.
If, after this, thou repent, I will forgive thee;
But if not, then I will take vengeance
On that which I most hate, hate of souls.
  Then thou gav'stst me thine own sweet hate,
  To love of souls so great a recompense as this.


5
Thou blind fool, Love, what dost thou to mine eyes
That they behold and see not what they see?
They know what beauty is, see where it lies,
And yet in their glass darkly doth it show thee.
For to thy sweet eyes, 'gainst thy sweet gaze a face doth lie,
Which in another's glass is better.
Now see what good turns eyes that way that doth show thee.
In the full height of heaven's grace dost thou sight
That thou mayst ascribe beauty to nothing;
Even so thy glass deceives thee.
  Take heed, love, of that glass that doth show thee;
  The better part of thee is in it, and the rest dead.


6
When my love swears that she is made of truth
I do believe her though I know she lies,
That she might think me some untutored youth,
Who for love of learning do inveigh
Against the gravity of the contrary.
For it is not easy to be straight
When contrary is your guide,
And so to be found in contradictory truth;
Therefore I do believe her though I know she lies,
That she might think me bold, eager to learn,
To thrust into that which she refuseth.
  Such a mind is my love that, though I thrust,
  He keeps his word, though I lose, he recovers.

O, call not me to justify the wrong
That thy unkindness lays upon my heart;
Wound me not with thine eye but with thy tongue;
Give not excuse for my crime
Unless my just cause was thine.
Be thou my advocate, O king, but do not plead
That I have done wrong, or that thou my wrong
Canst prove no better than my own doing.
O, change not what is proposed for change;
That is, what is proposed for changing,
Do not propose to me a worse state,
Nor I a better one; for better is it for me
To be acquainted with your just deserts,
Than to be told of thy own.
  If I am to be your advocate, be correct,
  And if you be to be my advocate, be true.

9
In faith, I do not love thee with mine eyes,
For they in thee a thousand errors note;
But 'tis my heart that loves what they despise,
And that love is my glory in these notes.
For to the outward is given the heart
Which in the inward is the same,
That to the outward is religious faith,
And from the heart doth his religious love derive.
Thou art therefore my all the greater treasure,
For to the parts is given the heart,
And in these is his religious respect.
  But what is religious love but that which doth live,
  Being made by the Author, in him doth religious love live.


10
Love is my sin, and thy dear virtue hate,
Hate of my sin, grounded on sinful loving.
O, but with mine compare thou thine own state,
And that more highly than with that which compares
In thee. Love is no other than mine own esteem,
And that in mine own esteem doth live
In thee as in myself, so that in thee
Thyself are the inward state and doth live.
This constant state doth often prove false,
For then love proves false and me false;
But in this we are both right and wrong.
  In him that lives well thou art assured
  Since well thou art assured of thyself being.


11
Lo, as a careful huswife runs to catch
One of her feathered creatures broke away,
Sets down her babe, and makes all swift dispatch
To her nest of fledglings, which are so swift
That they scarcely hear the musketry of the
bolt, though in a great wood with a hundred
bolts they do witness the slaughter.
Now, sweet woman, if you but had the skill,
To catch one of those fledglings did confound
Your swiftness, you should have had the joy of
seeing their lovely beauty cut away,
But, for their beauty so rare, you knew not
Where they came from, but thought them sold,
Plucked from the wild by your own skill, to be
copied elsewhere.
  Hence, that love, which so wondrously doth flourish
  As gold walks in love with silver, hath skill enough
  To know their owners well, and to know what is best.
